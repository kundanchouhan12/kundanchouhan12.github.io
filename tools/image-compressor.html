What is the Image Compressor?

The Image Compressor is a free, browser-based tool that reduces image file sizes while preserving as much visual quality as possible. By adjusting JPEG or WebP compression levels and optionally resizing to a maximum width, you can dramatically reduce file sizes for web publishing, email attachments, or mobile sharing. A real-time side-by-side comparison shows the original and compressed images simultaneously, so you can judge quality before downloading.

Web performance is closely tied to image size — large images are the single biggest cause of slow-loading websites. Google's PageSpeed Insights and Core Web Vitals scoring penalise pages with unoptimized images. By compressing images before uploading them to your website, blog, or e-commerce store, you improve load times, reduce bandwidth costs, and deliver a better experience to visitors on slow connections or mobile data.

How to Use — Step-by-Step

  1. Click the drop zone or drag your image file onto the page (JPG, PNG, WebP supported).
  2. The settings panel appears — choose your output format (JPEG, PNG, or WebP).
  3. Adjust the quality slider (1–100%) — lower values create smaller files with reduced quality.
  4. Optionally set a maximum width in pixels to resize the image while maintaining aspect ratio.
  5. Click Compress Image to generate the output.
  6. Review the before/after comparison and size savings badge, then click Download to save.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I compress an image without losing quality?
Use WebP format at 80–85% quality — it is 25–35% smaller than JPEG at the same visual quality. For JPEG, 75–80% quality is visually identical to 100% for most photos. Always resize to the actual display dimensions — a 4000px photo shown at 800px is wasting 5× the file size.
What is the ideal image size for a website?
Target sizes: Hero images under 200 KB, blog images under 100 KB, thumbnails under 50 KB. Keep image width under 1200px for most content areas. Google PageSpeed penalizes pages with oversized images and recommends serving in WebP format.
What is the difference between JPG, PNG, and WebP?
JPG: Lossy compression, small file, best for photos. PNG: Lossless compression, supports transparency, best for logos/screenshots. WebP: Modern format — 25–35% smaller than JPG, supports transparency and both lossy/lossless modes. Supported by all modern browsers.
What quality setting should I use for web images?
Hero/product images: 80–85%. Blog images: 75–80%. Thumbnails: 65–75%. At 75%+, quality loss is invisible to the human eye in most photos. Use the before/after comparison above to verify quality before downloading.
What is lossy vs lossless image compression?
Lossy (JPEG, WebP lossy) permanently removes some image data for smaller files — the result looks similar but is not pixel-identical. Lossless (PNG, WebP lossless) reduces file size without discarding data — decompression returns exact original pixels. Use lossy for photos; lossless for logos, icons, and text-heavy graphics.
How do I reduce an image to under 100 KB?
Upload your image above, choose JPEG or WebP, set quality to 75%, and check the compressed size. If still over 100 KB, use the Max Width slider to resize to 800–1200px width. Most 2–5 MB photos compress below 100 KB at 70–75% quality with no visible degradation.
How do I compress images for email?
For email: keep total attachment size under 1 MB. For embedded newsletter images, keep each under 100 KB. Use JPEG at 70–80% quality and resize to max 600px wide (standard email width). Avoid PNG for photos in email — JPEG is much smaller.
Are my images safe? Does this tool upload them?
Your images are never uploaded to any server. All compression uses the browser's Canvas API running locally on your device. Personal photos, business images, and private files stay completely private and are never transmitted anywhere.
